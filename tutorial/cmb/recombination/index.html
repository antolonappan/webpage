Recombination and the visibility function

The CMB exists because at some time in the universe's past, free electrons combined with protons to form neutral hydrogen, and photons that had been Thomson-scattering off those electrons suddenly found themselves in a transparent universe. This chapter computes the ionisation history xe(z)x_e(z)xe​(z) in detail, then constructs the visibility function g(τ)=κ˙(τ) e−κ(τ)g(\tau) = \dot\kappa(\tau)\, e^{-\kappa(\tau)}g(τ)=κ˙(τ)e−κ(τ) – the probability density that a CMB photon last scattered at conformal time τ\tauτ. The visibility function will appear in every line-of-sight integral in chapters 4-7.

Two pieces of physics determine xe(z)x_e(z)xe​(z). At high redshift, the photon-electron-proton plasma is in thermal equilibrium and chemical equilibrium gives the Saha equation. As the universe cools, the recombination rate cannot keep up with the expansion and the system departs from equilibrium; we then need a rate equation (Peebles 1968). Helium recombines somewhat earlier and has its own structure. Modern treatments (RECFAST, CosmoRec, HyRec) refine the original Peebles equation with a careful accounting of bound-state physics; we use the RECFAST formulation, which agrees with the others to better than 0.1%0.1\%0.1%.

Equilibrium: the Saha equation

In thermal equilibrium, the abundances of e−e^-e−, ppp, and HHH are related by chemical-potential balance:

μp+μe=μH.\mu_p + \mu_e = \mu_H.μp​+μe​=μH​.

Each species has a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution in the non-relativistic regime (T≪mic2T \ll m_i c^2T≪mi​c2):

ni=gi(miT2π)3/2e−(mi−μi)/T,n_i = g_i \Bigl(\frac{m_i T}{2\pi}\Bigr)^{3/2} e^{-(m_i - \mu_i)/T},ni​=gi​(2πmi​T​)3/2e−(mi​−μi​)/T,

with gig_igi​ the internal degeneracy (gp=ge=2g_p = g_e = 2gp​=ge​=2, gH=4g_H = 4gH​=4). Taking the ratio

nenpnH=gegpgH mempmH3/2 T3/2 e−(me+mp−mH)/T,\frac{n_e n_p}{n_H} = \frac{g_e g_p}{g_H}\, \frac{m_e m_p}{m_H}^{3/2}\, T^{3/2}\, e^{-(m_e + m_p - m_H)/T},nH​ne​np​​=gH​ge​gp​​mH​me​mp​​3/2T3/2e−(me​+mp​−mH​)/T,

and noting me+mp−mH=BHm_e + m_p - m_H = B_Hme​+mp​−mH​=BH​ (the binding energy of hydrogen, 13.6 eV13.6\,\mathrm{eV}13.6eV) and mp/mH≈1m_p/m_H \approx 1mp​/mH​≈1,

nenpnH=(meT2π)3/2 e−BH/T.\frac{n_e n_p}{n_H} = \Bigl(\frac{m_e T}{2\pi}\Bigr)^{3/2}\, e^{-B_H/T}.nH​ne​np​​=(2πme​T​)3/2e−BH​/T.

Defining the free-electron fraction xe=ne/nHtotx_e = n_e/n_H^\mathrm{tot}xe​=ne​/nHtot​ and using charge neutrality np=nen_p = n_enp​=ne​ together with conservation np+nH=nHtotn_p + n_H = n_H^\mathrm{tot}np​+nH​=nHtot​, we obtain the Saha equation for hydrogen:

xe21−xe=1nHtot (meT2π)3/2 e−BH/T.\frac{x_e^2}{1 - x_e} = \frac{1}{n_H^\mathrm{tot}}\,\Bigl(\frac{m_e T}{2\pi}\Bigr)^{3/2}\, e^{-B_H/T}.1−xe​xe2​​=nHtot​1​(2πme​T​)3/2e−BH​/T.

The right-hand side falls exponentially when TTT drops below BHB_HBH​, so xex_exe​ falls from ∼1\sim 1∼1 to ∼0\sim 0∼0 over a temperature range ΔT/T∼T/BH∼1/40\Delta T/T \sim T/B_H \sim 1/40ΔT/T∼T/BH​∼1/40 near T≈3000 KT \approx 3000\,\mathrm{K}T≈3000K (i.e. z≈1100z \approx 1100z≈1100).

The same logic applies to helium. There are two ionisation states (He++ and He+), each with its own Saha equation and binding energy (54.4 eV54.4\,\mathrm{eV}54.4eV for He++ →\to→ He+, 24.6 eV24.6\,\mathrm{eV}24.6eV for He+ →\to→ He). The first ionisation of helium completes around z≈6000z \approx 6000z≈6000; the second around z≈2500z \approx 2500z≈2500.

Saha fails when recombination becomes non-equilibrium

Saha works well at the onset of recombination, where most atoms are still ionised. It fails by the end. The reason is rate-limiting: every successful direct recombination e−+p→H+γe^- + p \to H + \gammae−+p→H+γ releases a Lyman-α\alphaα photon, and that photon has a very high probability of immediately reionising a nearby neutral hydrogen atom. The net recombination rate is suppressed by a huge factor.

The two escape channels are:

  1. Two-photon decay of the 2s2s2s state: H(2s)→H(1s)+2γH(2s) \to H(1s) + 2\gammaH(2s)→H(1s)+2γ, with Λ2s→1s=8.22 s−1\Lambda_{2s\to 1s} = 8.22\,\mathrm{s}^{-1}Λ2s→1s​=8.22s−1. Two photons share the energy, so neither is at the resonant wavelength.

  2. Cosmological redshift of Lyman-α\alphaα photons out of resonance, controlled by the expansion rate HHH.

The competition between these escape routes and the back-reaction (ionisation by Lyman-α\alphaα photons) yields the Peebles correction factor CrC_rCr​.

The Peebles equation

Consider hydrogen in three levels: ground state 1s1s1s, excited state 2s2s2s/2p2p2p (treated as one), and continuum (ionised). Effective recombination only goes through the n=2n = 2n=2 bottleneck. The rate of decrease of xex_exe​ is then

dxedt=−Cr [α(2)(Tm) nH xe2−β(Tr) (1−xe) e−hν2s/Tr],\frac{\mathrm{d}x_e}{\mathrm{d}t} = -C_r\,\Bigl[ \alpha^{(2)}(T_m)\, n_H\, x_e^2 - \beta(T_r)\, (1 - x_e)\, e^{-h\nu_{2s}/T_r}\Bigr],dtdxe​​=−Cr​[α(2)(Tm​)nH​xe2​−β(Tr​)(1−xe​)e−hν2s​/Tr​],

where α(2)\alpha^{(2)}α(2) is the case-B recombination coefficient (excited-state recombinations only – the ground-state ones are reabsorbed and don't count), β\betaβ is the photoionisation rate, TmT_mTm​ is the matter temperature, TrT_rTr​ is the radiation temperature. The Peebles factor is

Cr=1+K Λ2s→1s nH(1−xe)1+K (Λ2s→1s+β) nH(1−xe),K=λLyα38πH.C_r = \frac{1 + K\, \Lambda_{2s\to 1s}\, n_H (1 - x_e)} {1 + K\, (\Lambda_{2s\to 1s} + \beta)\, n_H (1 - x_e)}, \qquad K = \frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{Ly}\alpha}^3}{8\pi H}.Cr​=1+K(Λ2s→1s​+β)nH​(1−xe​)1+KΛ2s→1s​nH​(1−xe​)​,K=8πHλLyα3​​.

The numerator is the rate of effective recombination through both two-photon decay and cosmological redshift; the denominator includes the rate of reionisation by Lyman-α\alphaα photons.

RECFAST refines this with a multiplicative “fudge factor” of ≈1.125\approx 1.125≈1.125 (calibrated against multi-level atomic codes) and a pair of Gaussian corrections (AGauss1, AGauss2) that absorb residual high-precision corrections. Helium gets its own analogous rate equation, more involved because the singlet/triplet structure matters.

Matter temperature

The plasma is heated by photon scattering and cooled by the cosmological expansion. The dominant heating term is Compton coupling to the CMB photons:

dTmdt=−8σTaRTr4xe3mec (1+xe+fHe) (Tm−Tr)−2HTm.\frac{\mathrm{d}T_m}{\mathrm{d}t} = -\frac{8 \sigma_T a_R T_r^4 x_e}{3 m_e c\, (1 + x_e + f_\mathrm{He})}\,(T_m - T_r) - 2 H T_m.dtdTm​​=−3me​c(1+xe​+fHe​)8σT​aR​Tr4​xe​​(Tm​−Tr​)−2HTm​.

The first term tries to drive Tm→TrT_m \to T_rTm​→Tr​; the second is adiabatic cooling. Before recombination, Compton coupling wins decisively: Tm=TrT_m = T_rTm​=Tr​ to one part in 101010^{10}1010. After recombination, xex_exe​ drops by four orders of magnitude and the coupling weakens; TmT_mTm​ then falls as a−2a^{-2}a−2 (the −2HTm-2HT_m−2HTm​ term integrates to Tm∝1/a2T_m \propto 1/a^2Tm​∝1/a2, faster than the radiation Tr∝1/aT_r \propto 1/aTr​∝1/a, because matter is not relativistic).

Reionisation

After the first stars and quasars form (somewhere around z∼10z \sim 10z∼10-202020), their UV radiation re-ionises the intergalactic medium. The CMB sees this as a second scattering surface, of much lower amplitude than recombination but extending over a much longer time. We use the CAMB tanh model: xereion(z)x_e^\mathrm{reion}(z)xereion​(z) is parameterised as a smooth step centred on a redshift zrez_\mathrm{re}zre​ that is determined by matching the total optical depth τreion\tau_\mathrm{reion}τreion​ to its observed value (an input parameter of the code). Helium re-ionises around z≈3.5z \approx 3.5z≈3.5, contributing a second small step.

Optical depth and visibility function

Given xe(τ)x_e(\tau)xe​(τ), the Thomson opacity at conformal time τ\tauτ is

κ˙(τ)≡dκdτ=ne σT a=xe nHtot(τ=0) σTa2.\dot\kappa(\tau) \equiv \frac{\mathrm{d}\kappa}{\mathrm{d}\tau} = n_e\, \sigma_T\, a = \frac{x_e\, n_H^\mathrm{tot}(\tau=0)\, \sigma_T}{a^2}.κ˙(τ)≡dτdκ​=ne​σT​a=a2xe​nHtot​(τ=0)σT​​.

The optical depth from us back to time τ\tauτ is

κ(τ)=∫ττ0κ˙(τ′) dτ′.\kappa(\tau) = \int_\tau^{\tau_0} \dot\kappa(\tau')\, \mathrm{d}\tau'.κ(τ)=∫ττ0​​κ˙(τ′)dτ′.

The visibility function is the product

g(τ)=κ˙(τ) e−κ(τ).g(\tau) = \dot\kappa(\tau)\, e^{-\kappa(\tau)}.g(τ)=κ˙(τ)e−κ(τ).

It is a probability density: g(τ) dτg(\tau)\,\mathrm{d}\taug(τ)dτ is the probability that a CMB photon last scattered between τ\tauτ and τ+dτ\tau + \mathrm{d}\tauτ+dτ. By construction ∫0τ0g dτ=1\int_0^{\tau_0} g\, \mathrm{d}\tau = 1∫0τ0​​gdτ=1. The width of the peak around τ∗\tau_*τ∗​ is the thickness of the last-scattering surface; it controls Silk damping.

The code: solve_recombination

This is the main function. It integrates a three-variable ODE for xHx_HxH​, xHex_\mathrm{He}xHe​, and TmT_mTm​, with Saha initial conditions at high zzz.

Cell: solve_recombination – part 1 – Saha and RHS 
def solve_recombination(bg, params):
    """Solve the ionisation history x_e(z) using the RECFAST equations.

    Three-variable ODE for hydrogen ionisation x_H, helium ionisation x_He,
    and matter temperature T_m. Saha equilibrium at high z, switching to
    ODEs as each species departs from equilibrium.
    """
    T_cmb = bg['T_cmb']
    f_He  = bg['f_He']

    # Present-day hydrogen number density (m^-3)
    H100_SI      = 100 * 1e3 / Mpc_in_m
    rho_crit_100 = 3 * H100_SI**2 / (8 * np.pi * G)
    Nnow         = (1 - bg['Y_He']) * (params['omega_b_h2'] * rho_crit_100) / m_H

    # Hubble in SI for use inside the ODE
    omega_m = (params['omega_b_h2'] + params['omega_c_h2']) / params['h']**2
    a_eq    = (bg['grhog'] + bg['grhornomass']) / (bg['grhoc'] + bg['grhob'])
    z_eq    = 1.0 / a_eq - 1.0
    H0_SI   = bg['H0'] * c_SI / Mpc_in_m
    def Hz_SI(z):
        return hubble(1.0 / (1 + z), bg) * c_SI / Mpc_in_m

    # --- Saha equations for helium ---
    def saha_He2(z):
        """He++ -> He+ Saha: returns total x_e per H atom."""
        T   = T_cmb * (1 + z)
        rhs = (CR * T_cmb / (1 + z))**1.5 * np.exp(-CB1_He2 / T) / Nnow
        return 0.5 * (np.sqrt((rhs - 1 - f_He)**2
                              + 4 * (1 + 2 * f_He) * rhs) - (rhs - 1 - f_He))

    def saha_He1(z):
        """He+ -> He0 Saha: returns x_He = n(He+) / n_He."""
        T   = T_cmb * (1 + z)
        rhs = 4.0 * (CR * T_cmb / (1 + z))**1.5 * np.exp(-CB1_He1 / T) / Nnow
        x0  = 0.5 * (np.sqrt((rhs - 1)**2 + 4 * (1 + f_He) * rhs) - (rhs - 1))
        return min((x0 - 1.0) / f_He, 1.0)

    # --- RECFAST 3-variable RHS: dy/dz for y = [x_H, x_He, T_mat] ---
    def recfast_rhs(z, y):
        x_H   = max(y[0], 0.0)
        x_He  = max(y[1], 0.0)
        T_mat = max(y[2], 0.5)
        x     = x_H + f_He * x_He
        T_rad = T_cmb * (1 + z)
        n_H   = Nnow * (1 + z)**3
        n_He  = f_He * n_H
        Hz    = Hz_SI(z)

        # ---- f1: Hydrogen Peebles equation ----
        t4    = T_mat / 1e4
        Rdown = 1e-19 * 4.309 * t4**(-0.6166) / (1 + 0.6703 * t4**0.5300)
        Rup   = Rdown * (CR * T_mat)**1.5 * np.exp(-CDB / T_mat)
        K     = CK / Hz * (1.0
                + AGauss1 * np.exp(-((np.log(1 + z) - zGauss1) / wGauss1)**2)
                + AGauss2 * np.exp(-((np.log(1 + z) - zGauss2) / wGauss2)**2))
        fu    = RECFAST_fudge
        n_1s  = n_H * max(1 - x_H, 1e-30)
        f1    = ((x * x_H * n_H * Rdown
                  - Rup * (1 - x_H) * np.exp(-CL / T_mat))
                 * (1 + K * Lambda_2s1s * n_1s)
                 / (Hz * (1 + z) * (1.0/fu + K * Lambda_2s1s * n_1s / fu
                                    + K * Rup * n_1s)))

A few lines deserve commentary.

  • Rdown: the case-B recombination coefficient α(2)\alpha^{(2)}α(2) at matter temperature TmT_mTm​, fit to a power law in t4=Tm/104 Kt_4 = T_m/10^4\,\mathrm{K}t4​=Tm​/104K (Pequignot et al. 1991).

  • Rup: the photoionisation rate β\betaβ, obtained from α(2)\alpha^{(2)}α(2) by detailed balance, β=α(2) (meTr/(2π))3/2 e−Bn/Tr\beta = \alpha^{(2)}\, (m_e T_r/(2\pi))^{3/2}\, e^{-B_n/T_r}β=α(2)(me​Tr​/(2π))3/2e−Bn​/Tr​ with Bn=BH/n2B_n = B_H/n^2Bn​=BH​/n2 for n=2n = 2n=2.

  • K: the Peebles KKK factor λLyα3/(8πH)\lambda_\mathrm{Ly\alpha}^3/(8\pi H)λLyα3​/(8πH), with the two Gaussian corrections introduced by Hswitch refinements.

  • f1: the full Peebles equation Eq. with the Peebles factor CrC_rCr​ folded into the same expression.

                  * (T_mat - T_rad) / (Hz * (1 + z))
                  + 2 * T_mat / (1 + z))

        return [f1, f2, f3]

The matter-temperature branch (f3) implements Eq. with a switch: if the Compton timescale tC=(CTTr4x)−1t_C = (C_T T_r^4 x)^{-1}tC​=(CT​Tr4​x)−1 is much shorter than the Hubble time, TmT_mTm​ is essentially locked to TrT_rTr​ and we use an analytic asymptotic; otherwise we integrate the full ODE. This avoids stiffness during the tight-coupling era.

Cell: solve_recombination – part 3 – driver 
    # --- Build z grid ---
    z_start, z_end, nz = 10000, 0, 20000
    z_arr   = np.linspace(z_start, z_end, nz + 1)

    xHe_arr = np.ones(nz + 1)

    # --- Phase 1: Saha equilibrium ---
    he_ode_idx = None
    for i, z in enumerate(z_arr):
        if z > 8000.0:
            xH_arr[i] = 1.0; xHe_arr[i] = 1.0
            xe_total[i] = 1.0 + 2.0 * f_He
        elif z > 5000.0:
            xH_arr[i] = 1.0; xHe_arr[i] = 1.0
            xe_total[i] = saha_He2(z)
        elif z > 3500.0:
            xH_arr[i] = 1.0; xHe_arr[i] = 1.0
            xe_total[i] = 1.0 + f_He
        elif z > 0:
            x_He = saha_He1(z)
            xHe_arr[i] = x_He
            xH_arr[i]  = 1.0
            xe_total[i] = 1.0 + f_He * x_He
            if x_He < 0.99:
                he_ode_idx = i
                break
        else:
            break
    if he_ode_idx is None:
        he_ode_idx = len(z_arr) - 1

    # --- Phase 2: 3-variable ODE from He departure to z = 0 ---
    z_ode = z_arr[he_ode_idx:]
    z_ode = z_ode[z_ode >= 0]
    Tmat_arr = T_cmb * (1.0 + z_arr)
    if len(z_ode) > 1:

              T_cmb * (1 + z_ode[0])]
        sol = integrate.solve_ivp(
            recfast_rhs,
            [z_ode[0], z_ode[-1]], y0,
            t_eval=z_ode, method='Radau', rtol=1e-6, atol=1e-10,
            max_step=5.0,
        )
        n_sol = min(sol.y.shape[1], len(z_arr) - he_ode_idx)
        idx   = he_ode_idx + n_sol
        xH_arr[he_ode_idx:idx]   = sol.y[0, :n_sol]
        xHe_arr[he_ode_idx:idx]  = sol.y[1, :n_sol]
        Tmat_arr[he_ode_idx:idx] = sol.y[2, :n_sol]
    xe_total[he_ode_idx:] = xH_arr[he_ode_idx:] + f_He * xHe_arr[he_ode_idx:]
    return z_arr, xe_total, Tmat_arr

The driver runs in two phases. Phase 1 walks down the redshift grid applying Saha equilibrium in the regions where it is valid; this is fast and stable. As soon as helium first departs from equilibrium (x_He < 0.99), phase 2 hands off to the full three-variable Radau ODE solver, which handles the hydrogen recombination transition automatically. We use Radau (implicit) because the system is stiff – the Compton coupling drives the matter temperature very fast.

The code: build_thermodynamics

Once we have xe(z)x_e(z)xe​(z) we add the reionisation layer, compute conformal time, the opacity, the optical depth, and the visibility function. The full function below also computes the sound horizon and Silk damping scale, which we will need in chapter 5.

Cell: build_thermodynamics 
def build_thermodynamics(bg, params):
    """Build thermodynamic tables: opacity, optical depth, visibility function,
    plus derived recombination quantities (z_*, tau_*, r_s, k_D, ...)."""
    z_arr, xe_arr, Tmat_rec = solve_recombination(bg, params)

    # Reionisation layer: CAMB tanh model
    f_He        = bg['f_He']
    delta_z     = 0.5
    he_reion_z  = 3.5
    he_reion_dz = 0.4
    he_reion_zstart = he_reion_z + 5 * he_reion_dz
    f_re        = 1.0 + f_He
    z_rev, xe_rev = z_arr[::-1], xe_arr[::-1]

    def build_reion_xe(z_eval, z_re):
        z_reion_start = z_re + 8 * delta_z
        x_e_freeze    = np.interp(z_reion_start, z_rev, xe_rev)
        window_var_mid   = (1 + z_re)**1.5
        window_var_delta = 1.5 * (1 + z_re)**0.5 * delta_z
        xod = np.clip((window_var_mid - (1 + z_eval)**1.5) / window_var_delta, -100, 100)
        x_H_reion = (f_re - x_e_freeze) * (np.tanh(xod) + 1) / 2 + x_e_freeze
        xod_he    = np.clip((he_reion_z - z_eval) / he_reion_dz, -100, 100)
        x_he_extra = np.where(z_eval < he_reion_zstart,
                              f_He * (np.tanh(xod_he) + 1) / 2, 0.0)
        return x_H_reion + x_he_extra

    def compute_reion_optical_depth(z_re):
        z_reion_start = z_re + 8 * delta_z
        def integrand(z):
            a = 1.0 / (1 + z)
            return build_reion_xe(z, z_re) * bg['akthom'] * dtau_da(a, bg)
        return integrate.quad(integrand, 0, z_reion_start, limit=200)[0]

    # Solve for z_re matching the target reionisation tau
    def tau_residual(z_re):
        return compute_reion_optical_depth(z_re) - params['tau_reion']
    z_re = optimize.brentq(tau_residual, 2.0, 30.0, xtol=1e-8, rtol=1e-8)

    # Refine the z grid around z_re
    z_reion_lo = max(0.01, z_re - 8 * delta_z)
    z_reion_hi = z_re + 8 * delta_z
    z_dense    = np.linspace(z_reion_hi, z_reion_lo, 200)
    mask       = (z_arr > z_reion_hi) | (z_arr < z_reion_lo)
    z_new      = np.sort(np.concatenate([z_arr[mask], z_dense]))[::-1]
    xe_new     = np.interp(z_new[::-1], z_arr[::-1], xe_arr[::-1])[::-1]
    Tmat_new   = np.interp(z_new[::-1], z_arr[::-1], Tmat_rec[::-1])[::-1]
    z_arr, xe_arr, Tmat_rec = z_new, xe_new, Tmat_new

    # Final x_e: max of recombination + reionisation
    xe_final = np.maximum(build_reion_xe(z_arr, z_re), xe_arr)

    # Conformal time grid (monotonically increasing in tau)
    a_arr   = 1.0 / (1 + z_arr)
    tau_arr = conformal_time(a_arr, bg)

    # Opacity, optical depth, visibility
    opacity     = xe_final * bg['akthom'] / a_arr**2
    tau_optical = -np.flip(integrate.cumulative_trapezoid(
        np.flip(opacity), np.flip(tau_arr), initial=0))
    exptau     = np.exp(-tau_optical)
    visibility = opacity * exptau

    # Assemble the thermo dictionary
    thermo = {
        'z_arr': z_arr, 'a_arr': a_arr, 'tau_arr': tau_arr,
        'xe': xe_final, 'Tmat': Tmat_rec,
        'opacity': opacity, 'tau_optical': tau_optical,
        'exptau': exptau, 'visibility': visibility,
        'z_reion': z_re,
    }
    thermo['opacity_interp']    = interpolate.CubicSpline(tau_arr, opacity)
    thermo['exptau_interp']     = interpolate.CubicSpline(tau_arr, exptau)
    thermo['visibility_interp'] = interpolate.CubicSpline(tau_arr, visibility)

    # Baryon sound speed
    dlnT_dln_a = np.gradient(np.log(np.maximum(Tmat_rec, 1e-30)), np.log(a_arr))
    barssc = barssc0 * (1.0 - 0.75 * bg['Y_He'] + (1.0 - bg['Y_He']) * xe_arr)
    cs2_b  = np.maximum(barssc * Tmat_rec * (1.0 - dlnT_dln_a / 3.0), 0.0)
    thermo['cs2_b'] = cs2_b

    # Peak of g(tau): the surface of last scattering
    peak_idx = np.argmax(visibility)
    thermo['z_star']   = z_arr[peak_idx]
    thermo['tau_star'] = tau_arr[peak_idx]
    a_star             = 1.0 / (1.0 + thermo['z_star'])

    # Sound horizon r_s and Silk damping scale k_D
    thermo['r_s'] = sound_horizon(a_star, bg)
    a_grid       = np.linspace(a_arr[0], a_star, 5000)
    R            = 0.75 * bg['grhob'] * a_grid / bg['grhog']
    dtauda_grid  = dtau_da(a_grid, bg)
    kappa_dot    = np.maximum(np.interp(a_grid, a_arr, xe_final) * bg['akthom'] / a_grid**2, 1e-30)
    integrand_D  = (R**2 + 16.0*(1.0+R)/15.0) / (6.0*(1.0+R)**2 * kappa_dot) * dtauda_grid
    thermo['k_D'] = 1.0 / np.sqrt(np.trapz(integrand_D, a_grid))

    # FWHM widths of the visibility peaks (needed by make_k_grid / make_tau_grid)
    fwhm_to_sigma = 2.0 * np.sqrt(2.0 * np.log(2.0))
    half_max  = visibility[peak_idx] / 2.0
    tau_left  = np.interp(half_max, visibility[:peak_idx+1], tau_arr[:peak_idx+1])
    tau_right = np.interp(half_max, visibility[peak_idx:][::-1], tau_arr[peak_idx:][::-1])
    thermo['delta_tau_rec'] = (tau_right - tau_left) / fwhm_to_sigma

    z_rev, tau_rev = z_arr[::-1], tau_arr[::-1]
    thermo['tau_reion'] = np.interp(z_re, z_rev, tau_rev)
    thermo['delta_tau_reion'] = abs(
        np.interp(z_re + 6*delta_z, z_rev, tau_rev)
        - np.interp(max(0.01, z_re - 6*delta_z), z_rev, tau_rev)) / fwhm_to_sigma

    return thermo

Reading the code.

The function does five things in order:

  1. Compute the bare recombination history with solve_recombination.

  2. Layer on a reionisation tanh model whose midpoint zrez_\mathrm{re}zre​ is solved for by Brent's method to match the input params['tau_reion'].

  3. Convert the zzz grid to a τ\tauτ grid via conformal_time(a_arr, bg) from chapter 1.

  4. Build κ˙\dot\kappaκ˙, integrate it backwards to get κ(τ)\kappa(\tau)κ(τ), and form g(τ)g(\tau)g(τ).

  5. Compute the sound horizon rs(τ∗)r_s(\tau_*)rs​(τ∗​) and Silk damping scale kDk_DkD​ – both as integrals against the recombination history. These become inputs for the spectrum chapter.

Why recombination is sharp

The visibility-peak width is set by the competition between recombination rate ∝nHα(2)\propto n_H \alpha^{(2)}∝nH​α(2) and expansion rate HHH. Their ratio crosses unity exponentially fast because α(2)\alpha^{(2)}α(2) varies as a low power of TTT while photoionisation is exponentially suppressed by e−BH/Te^{-B_H/T}e−BH​/T. The Saha-equation logic gives ΔT/T∼T/BH∼1/40\Delta T/T \sim T/B_H \sim 1/40ΔT/T∼T/BH​∼1/40; converted to redshift, Δz≈z∗/40≈30\Delta z \approx z_*/40 \approx 30Δz≈z∗​/40≈30. The full visibility function is somewhat wider (Δz≈80\Delta z \approx 80Δz≈80) because the freeze-out tail extends recombination. This narrowness is what makes “last scattering” an almost-well-defined surface.
